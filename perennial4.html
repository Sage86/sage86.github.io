Casestudy: Perennial

Perennial is a vintage clothing e-commerce app for consumers aged 25-34yrs. The name combines Millenial, with the Oxford dictionary definition - lasting or existing for a long or apparently infinite time; enduring or continually recurring.

Taking into account that certain fashion trends tend to make a resurgence every decade or so, thus implying that the clothing that Millenials wore in their youth is now seen as popular and new by Gen Z.

Users of Perennial are able to upload items to sell, and buy from other users of the app.


My Process


Research

Problem: Too many uploaded images only show the garment on a hanger, and not when worn.

Goal: Develop the Upload process in greater depth to encourage the seller to upload images of the item of clothing they want to sell as it would look on the body, resulting in a better representation of how it looks when worn.

Firstly, I conducted a competetive analysis of two widely used fashion buy/sell apps used today - Depop and Vinted. Each app appears to attract a different age range of consumers.


Ideation

Focusing on one user flow - upload an item to sell, I created a user journey map and some basic sketches of initial UI and UX navigation.

I also observed vintage T-shirt e-commerce apps - how many search results on each screen, the size of each image, if the text is on overlay or appears underneath.


Wireframing

Instead of the process of uploading being displayed as a series of horizontal scrolling from step to step, I designed the process as a vertical one for the bulk of each section (uploading, categorising, packaging and payment). Users are swayed from just taking photos of the clothing item on a hanger.

Tapping each tab with an arrow results in another dropdown sub-menu with information of greater detail relative to the tab’s heading.


Design

The decision to keep the upload box black, obscuring the blank photos, was eventually scrapped, in favour of keeping the blank photos visible, and transforming the upload box into an overlay. The top navigation bars are fixed for every screen. Shopping basket reminds the user of the amount of items that have been saved. Horizontal scrolling per item to mimic the filing through clothing rails in a shop.

Regarding the colour palette, I had considered using autumnal colours that were leaning more towards the grunge scene. I didn’t that pop art colours would be a good idea for an app like this. The initial background colour was going to be a royal blue, but combining that with the yellow resembled Blockbuster Video.


Prototyping

This prototype follows the user journey of logging in and uploading an item of clothing to sell.


Conclusion

Next, I would design more screens focusing on other user journeys within the app - leave a review, contact Customer Service, etc.

I would also plan to generate a more in-depth prototype, and test this on more users. - leaving reviews, receiving messages / notifications from other buyers against items in e-shop.
